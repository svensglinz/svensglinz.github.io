Research Question

My bachelor thesis summarizes and investigates the issue of large spikes in margin requirements in financial markets with a specific focus on centrally cleared derivative markets. As the market turmoil during the Covid Outbreak in March 2020 endured, margins for derivative contracts rose sharply at most clearing houses which brought about new regulatory scrutiny about how such margins behave during crises. Since the Great Financial Crisis in 2008, derivative markets underwent significant reforms, among other mandatory clearing requirements for ETD and most standardized OTC transatcions. Central clearing of these contracts removes the buildup of large, sometimes uncolalteralized bilateral exposures between market participants and bundles all risk in one entity, the CCP. Through prudent risk management and the daily collection of margin from its participants, the CCP thus virtually eliminates counterparty risk between market participants as it is the sole conterparty to every derivative contract. However, to shield itself from market risks, real-time margin calculations at CCPs can transform credit risks into liquidity risks, where a sudden spike in financial market volatility leads the CCP to quickly adjust its margins upwards which can lead to liquidity outflows at members in already stressed times. In the works case, this can lead to a self-enforcing downward spiral, where members have to sell liquid assets to meed their liquidity neeeds which further pushes volatility up and assset values down and can ultimately end in a crisis.
